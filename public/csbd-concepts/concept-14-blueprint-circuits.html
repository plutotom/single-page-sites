Compulsive Sexual Behavior Disorder (CSBD)

A brief psychoeducation guide

What is CSBD? A pattern of sexual urges or behaviors that are difficult to control and continue despite negative consequences. Examples may include pornography use, masturbation, cybersex, or sexual behavior with others.

Big Idea

High sexual desire alone is not enough; CSBD involves loss of control and meaningful distress or impairment.

How Researchers Explain CSBD

  • Hypersexual Disorder: proposed DSM framing of high drive plus poor control
  • Behavioral Addiction Model: craving/reward cycle and continued use despite harm
  • Obsessive-Compulsive Model: behavior may reduce anxiety or tension
  • Impulse Control Model: difficulty resisting urges
  • Moral Incongruence / PPMI: distress from values conflict does not necessarily indicate CSBD

Signs and Symptoms

  • Repeated failure to control sexual urges or behaviors
  • Sexual behavior becomes central in life
  • Multiple failed attempts to cut back
  • Continues despite harm or consequences
  • Causes distress or impairment for 6 months or more

Why It May Happen

  • Trauma history
  • Emotional dysregulation
  • Depression or anxiety
  • Family history of addiction
  • Easy access to sexual content online

How Common Is It?

  • Rates vary across studies
  • Some estimates suggest about 5% globally
  • More commonly reported in men
  • Many people do not seek treatment

What Tends To Happen Over Time

  • Often begins in late adolescence
  • Can become chronic without treatment
  • Therapy and support may help reduce symptoms

Cultural + Spiritual Considerations

  • Sexual norms differ across cultures
  • Moral or religious shame can increase distress
  • Distress from values conflict alone does not equal CSBD

Treatment + Support

  • Individual therapy and relapse-prevention planning
  • Support groups for accountability and connection
  • Mindfulness and grounding skills for urges
  • Medication may help in some cases
